The Pendulum Swings Back :
The Rise of Masculinity and Conservatism in the West

Over the past year, there's been a noticeable shift towards traditional masculinity and conservatism across the Western world. Instead of a random occurrence, this trend reflects deeper economic, cultural, and sociological forces shaping modern life.

Note: This article isn't made to promote or endorse any particular ideology, but to explore and explain the societal shift taking place in the West.

Civilizations evolve through cycles of extremes, oscillating like a pendulum between opposing ideologies to maintain balance. The ’80s and ’90s were marked by globalization, economic booms, and a focus on traditional family structures (hyper-financiarization of the economy, Wall Street culture ("greed is good"), hypermasculine icons -Schwarzenegger- and action movies -Rambo-, relocating production to third-world countries with little regard for negative externalities, etc...). Masculinity, tied to economic success and societal stability, reached its peak during this time. Over the Last Decade, movements like gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights, and DEI (Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion) emerged as a response to the rigid, patriarchal norms inherited from the past century. However, when any societal shift moves too far in one direction (as the latter arguably did), it inevitably provokes a counterreaction to restore some type of balance. In my view, today’s rise of masculinity and conservatism is society’s attempt to rebalance the scales.

The resurgence of masculine values is also tied to the current economic climate. In times of uncertainty, stagnation and fear (especially as the West faces growing competition from China, Russia, and other Eastern powers), societies unconsciously gravitate toward characteristics unconsciously associated with masculinity: survival, strength and endurance. It might sound ironic but I believe this trend is also reflected in the growing popularity of beards, weightlifting and combat sports among men. Whether consciously or not, these symbolic gestures are ways to get prepared for the perceived tough times ahead. These trends also manifest themselves in the cultural appeal of controversial politicians (like Trump or Orban) and media figures (like Joe Rogan or the Tate brothers) who represent and/or promote self-reliance and traditional masculinity.

There is also a backlash against what many in the West perceive as the imposition of leftist/collectivist policies. Such policies may have been more broadly accepted during periods of economic growth. But in the post-pandemic era, marked by rising inflation (over 8% on average in most western countries in 2023), stagnating growth and rising inequality, the "each man on his own" mindset becomes the default attitude. The lower your income and the higher the uncertainty about the future, the less you are willing to share risks & resources with others.

This attitude becomes even more evident as mass immigration becomes a major concern. As Western societies become increasingly diverse, studies suggest that people are less likely to share resources or risks as a group becomes larger and more culturally heterogeneous. This dynamic fuels the rise of anti-immigration rhetoric and policies that resonate with conservative ideologies, which prioritize national identity, cultural cohesion, and masculine strength.

We are also living through exponential economic and societal changes driven by technological progress. Many sectors are being disrupted and the uncertainties about an AI-dominated future are growing. Social media is flooded with information about new technologies that may replace humans or render certain roles obsolete (the World Economic Forum predicts that 14 million jobs globally could be lost to automation and robotization by 2027). So, not only is the pace of change accelerating, but we're also witnessing it unfold in real time, which heightens anxiety. In this context, conservatism and right-wing ideologies, which promise order, security, and a restoration of traditional roles, become increasingly reassuring and appealing.

History has shown us that unchecked swings in either direction, whether toward hypermasculinity or extreme progressivism, can destabilize societies. The real danger now is the risk of over-correcting: when the pendulum swings too far & ends up creating new imbalances. The challenge ahead for Western Societies is to navigate this new "masculine" era in a balanced way. Without falling into extremist behaviors or worse, a regressive ideology that glorifies unchecked machismo and extreme individualism, one that excludes marginalized voices and seeks to dominate the weak.
